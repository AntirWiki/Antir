Wynn was nine years old when they first saw [[World Lore/Gods/Death]].

They were at their grandmother's bedside, holding her hand as she shared her last words with Wynn's mother. Wynn looked out the window to their family ranch, and saw a beast unfamiliar to them. Pale white flanks and an equally colorless mane stood out from the roan mares kept by their family. They swear that the horse made eye contact with them as they looked out the window, but before they could react, a soft gasp left their grandmother's mouth as she left this world. When Wynn turned back to the window, the strange creature was gone.

As the years went by, Wynn thought often about this encounter. Setting off at age 19 to find their fortune on the road, many sleepless nights were spent considering the pale white horse. By this point, they had come to the conclusion that the being they'd seen was a spirit. And not just any: they knew they had seen Death herself. Being somewhat well versed in the spiritual, Wynn assumed that they saw Death as a horse since that is what they were most familiar with. When, on their travels, they mentioned this to a village Wisdom, the woman replied no.

Death is a horse.

None know why, she said, when so many spirits are so mutable in form, Death takes a single form to all who see her. Without fail, when one of the living sees Death, they see a pale white horse looking back at them. Neither could the wisdom say why some could see Death as she visited another, but most could not.

The mystery stayed with Wynn as they found the city and enlisted in the King's cavalry. And when the would-be kings began to vie for a crown, Wynn gained a new familiarity with death.

Never on the many battlefields did Wynn see the pale white horse. But as brothers and sisters in arms fell beside them, they could feel her presence. Hear her footfalls on the bloodied earth. Feel her breath on their neck as they cradled a dying bannerman in their arms.

When titans clashed and a crown was won, as swords and spells scarred the earth and bodies covered the scorched battlefield like a carpet, Wynn finally saw the mare again.

They stood over the aftermath, watching the fires die down and the last scraps of armies retreat. They stood, despite the fact that their body lay on the ground below them, an arrow through the heart. How they stood they did not know, and did not care. For as they looked out, they saw a pale white horse moving across the battlefield, and they saw the spirits of the fallen stand from their bodies to walk alongside her. Wynn knew the look of a wary horse, so despite Death's calm, measured steps, they could see her distress at the magnitude of the task set before her. The sheer number of souls set astray in this place.

And so Wynn began to walk. To the bodies of slain countrymen, friend or foe alike, extending a hand to help them to their feet. Offering a kind word or a steady arm when needed. And when half of the fallen stood behind them, they approached Death, who had collected the other half. As they looked their lifelong companion in the eye, they spoke four words.

"Where is your rider?"

None know why Death appears the same to all who see her. But without fail, when one of the living sees Death, they see a pale white horse and a cavalier, face shrouded by a helm, riding or walking alongside her.
