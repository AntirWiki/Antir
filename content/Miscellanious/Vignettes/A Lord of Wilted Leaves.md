In Tir na Nog, the realm of spirits, perspective is everything. They feywild in particular is known as the realm of exaggeration and extremes, taking the things mortals perceive and heightening them to a near-unrecognizable degree. 

When the Lord of Gilded Leaves, archfey member of the Spring Court, gains not one but *two* new perspectives, it changes him on a fundamental level. 

Seeing through the eyes of a twisted Ninyva O'Niamh, he hears her thoughts and her prayers. The idea that these mortals - wizards, she calls them - can corrupt, capture, and weaken the fae is deeply unsettling to the Lord. He begins to consider that perhaps a more meaningful effort is in order. He may need to warn others in his court about this threat, at the very least.

It is at this point that Kyrillos, soul half-digested by the fae, sees an opening. Using months' worth of saved strength, he reaches out to his captor. 

"It is not only the wizards, my Lord. *All* mortals would seek to corrupt you, use you, and destroy your works."

The Lord of Gilded Leaves ponders this. Could it really be true? As a fae of a more herbal domain, he has had little interaction with mortals - mainly the occasional supplications for aid with plagues or harvests. Surely these weak little creatures aren't such a threat.

It is at this point that he witnesses his most recent creation, the twisted witch of vine and storm, be rendered powerless by four mortals, souls touched by beast, dragon, god, and arcana. She is struck down by weavings of magical, divine, and martial power unlike anything the Lord of Gilded Leaves has ever witnessed.

In the Lord's moment of shock, the voice of Kyrillos worms its way back in. "You see? They all work against you and your kind, mages and beastkin alike. Even their servants, Saints and Gods, seek your undoing. There is only one way they can be stopped."

The archfey, distraught, is drawn in. "How can we survive this?" he asks the captive lich.

"You are a creature of Spring," Kyrillos observes, "but you know of the other seasons, do you not? There is one where all life that has overgrown its place withers and dies, rotting away to make room for a new beginning. I have studied death more than any other in this realm, and I can show you how to wield it to cull the mortals that would overcrowd this world."

The fae considers this. He knows that this strange soul is right - the world grows crowded, with no need for the new growth of Spring. The heat and harvest of Summer would only strengthen these mortals, and Winter, with its harsh winds and barren soils, would kill him just as quickly. But he knows of one season that fertilizes his soil with rot, and clears space for new meadows and forests to grow.

"I accept, specter. Teach me the ways of Autumn."

Over a hundred years later, the new Lord of Autumn begins his campaign against the mortal world.
